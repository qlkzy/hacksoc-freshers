What is HackSoc?

HackSoc is the computer science society here at the University of York, although you don't need to be a computer science student to participate. We run a mixture of academic and social events, providing a way for people to learn about things they're interested in, which may not necessarily be covered by the actual course.

Whilst plans for the next academic year are a bit hazy at the moment, we will be running an intermediate-level Python course in the Spring term, and hope to run some introductory courses in the Autumn term.

You can find more information on our website, hacksoc.org.

What events do you run?

We run a mixture of academic and social events. Our academic events consist of lectures and seminars, which anyone can ask to run. We try to run one or two courses each term as well. Our staple social events are boardgames evenings and visiting pubs, although we're considering branching out.

We're also looking into the possibility of guest lectures and trips for next year, although currently these are very provisional.

How can I get in touch?

You can email the committee directly at hack@yusu.org, subscribe to our mailing list, or join us on IRC in #cs-york on freenode. If you've never used IRC or a mailing list before don't worry, you will soon pick it up.

What should I bring?

YUSU provide a checklist of things to bring, so we shall only include the more essential things here:

  • Government-issued photographic ID (if you have some)
  • Spare passport photos
  • For international students: visa and any documentation
  • Clothes
  • Towels
  • Toiletries
  • Duvet
  • Pillows
  • Bed sheets
  • Cutlery
  • Crockery
  • Pots and pans
  • Cooking utensils
  • Cleaning materials
  • Enough food to survive for a few days

The kitchens do get cleaned once a week, but you are expected to keep it clean for the majority of the time, and having your kitchen be consistently messy can get you a fine!

There is no need to bring food with you, as there are many places fairly nearby (the Morrisons is a favourite spot for students), so you can always go out and get some after unpacking the rest of your stuff. Many flats all pool together and get shopping delivered, so if you don't feel like trekking to the shops every fortnight or so, perhaps talk to your flatmates about that.

If you're in catered accommodation, you can get some general information about that on the Commercial Services website, or directly look up the catering outlets.

What do I do on moving in day?

Firstly, make sure you know which car park is closest to your college, as carrying your stuff all over the place is not fun. There are lots of car parks, so you should be able to find one fairly close to your new home. Once you arrive, there should be a plethora of helpers wandering around, guiding people to and fro, and helping people transport their things. You'll want to go get your keys and university card, and someone will probably grab you and take you to where that's happening as soon as you get out of the car.

Once you have your keys, go find your room and proceed to move things over there. Again, there will be people to help you if you get lost, so don't be afraid to ask. Furthermore, there are trolleys (£10 deposit required), so if you're lucky you might be able to get one before they are all gone.

Leave your door open, and go introduce yourself to your flatmates. Remember: they're just as confused as you are. Later in the day, your STYCs (Second and Third Year Contacts) will arrive, these are two people who are assigned to look after your flat for freshers' week, and are typically very nice people. Their job is to answer any questions you might have, so ask away.

To finish the day, there will be some sort of college welcome event, which your STYCs will take you to.

Who will look after me?

Fortunately, the university and YUSU are rather used to people being lost when they arrive, and so provide many points of contact if you need help.

During freshers week you will have two STYCs (Second and Third Year Contacts), whose job is to provide any help you need during that week. You will also be assigned a supervisor, an academic in your department who will look after you for all of your time at York. Finally, your college will have a welfare team, YUSU has a welfare team, and there are other societies like Nightline who also provide support.

We all know that starting a totally new life can be scary and confusing, so if you need any help at all, don't be afraid to ask anyone. Even if they can't solve your problem, they should be able to point you to someone who can.

What are the colleges?

For organisational purposes, the university is divided into a number of undergraduate colleges, and one postgraduate college (Wentworth). However, unlike many universities, which college you end up in isn't terribly important. You will be a member of a college for your time at York, and the major thing this affects is where you live in your first year. Colleges do run events such as film nights, trips, toasties, and so on, but in general, anyone from any college can turn up to any event.

How do I apply for accommodation, and where should I live?

Once you have accepted an unconditional offer (that is, you have your results and confirmed that York is where you'll be going), you will receive an email giving you access to the online application system for accommodation. On results day, the emails are sent out over a 48 hour period randomly, so you may not get yours until the next day, but don't worry. Once you make your application, you'll receive a room offer within three days, after which you must pay a deposit to confirm it. If you don't confirm within a few days, your offer will be withdrawn, and you'll have to apply again.

Which college you apply to depends mostly on what you'll be studying, and what you want. Some colleges offer en-suite rooms, some offer catering, some are on the main campus, some are on the Heslington East campus. Hes East houses Goodricke and Langwith colleges, and also the departments of Computer Science; Theatre, Film, and Television; and Law and Management.

Where are things on campus?

There are two (well, three) campuses at York. The main campus, also known as Heslington West, the Heslington East campus, and King's Manor, which is sometimes used by the Archaeology and History departments. The two Heslington campuses are about ten minutes apart by foot, and there are free buses between them.

Most things are on the main campus, and Hes East has Goodricke and Langwith colleges; the departments of Computer Science, Theatre Film and Television, Law and Management; and the Langwith bar, The Glasshouse. There are two Costcutters on campus, one in Market Square (next to Vanbrugh College) and one at Halifax College. The university library is also next to both Alcuin and Vanbrugh colleges.

You can find a campus map on the university website, and you shall doubtlessly be provided with one shortly after you arrive.

Where are things in York?

The university itself is situated in Heslington, a village on the outskirts of York. Heslington features a post office, a Co-operative, and a few pubs. The city centre itself is about a 40 to 50 minute walk from main campus, and on the way is the Foss Islands Retail Park, which has a Morrisons and a few other shops.

What is YUSU?

YUSU is the University of York Students' Union, and is responsible for representing students in the university as a whole, for managing the societies, sports clubs, and also run two of the campus bars. For most students, YUSU is perhaps a mysterious organisation often spoken of, but that doesn't really seem to affect them directly, but if you get involved with societies, welfare, representation, or the like, you will quickly learn how much YUSU does.

Basically, if you have any problems during your time as a student at York, YUSU can help you.

What are societies and clubs?

Societies are student-run organisations which are a part of YUSU. There are many societies, catering to most interests. Many societies charge a membership fee, but this is typically a low amount, like £4. HackSoc does not charge a membership fee currently. Any student can join any society, so don't be afraid to just turn up to something and try it out, most don't worry about membership fees for people just having a look.

Clubs are like societies, but are for sports. Again, there are a lot of clubs, and you can try any of them out. Club membership fees tend to be a bit higher than for societies, and also may have per-session fees, but this is because most clubs bring in a trained instructor to run sessions.

In the Freshers' Fair at the end of freshers' week, all societies and clubs have the opportunity to run a stall and advertise themselves, so you will have plenty of opportunity to learn about ones which may interest you. Try out as many as you can!
